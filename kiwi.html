Citizen science & conservation · NZ

Why kiwi are disappearing — and who’s tracking it

New Zealanders are logging thousands of wildlife sightings every year through citizen science platforms. The question this project set out to answer: do those sightings actually impact conservation efforts? Scroll through the story to see what the data reveals — then see how it compares to where DOC and community groups are actually operating.

When someone spots a kiwi — or a stoat, a rat, a possum — and logs it on iNaturalist NZ (Mātaki Taiao), DOC’s recommended recording platform, that observation goes through a community verification process. Once enough people agree on the species ID, it reaches “Research Grade” and is automatically shared to GBIF — the Global Biodiversity Information Facility — a worldwide database that aggregates verified sightings from citizen scientists, researchers, and museum collections into a single, freely queryable system. The pipeline from public observation to global database is one-way and automatic.

This map was built by calling the GBIF API directly — the same data pipeline any researcher or agency could connect to. Every dot represents a real, verified sighting. Scroll to layer in the predators that share — and threaten — the kiwi’s habitat, and see what the data reveals about where the pressure is greatest.

Note: kiwi sightings in Auckland and Wellington are likely concentrated at sanctuaries and zoos — Zealandia (Wellington) and Auckland Zoo in particular — and may not reflect wild population distribution in those regions.

70 million years of isolation

Kiwi evolved in complete isolation from land predators. Over millions of years they lost the ability to fly, began nesting on the ground, and developed nostrils at the tip of their beak — the only bird in the world to do so — to sniff out invertebrates in the soil at night. Roughly the size of a domestic cat, they are more closely related to ostriches and emus than to any other New Zealand bird. They had no reason to fear what lived beside them, because for 70 million years, nothing threatened them.

Then came the possum

Brought from Australia in 1837 for the fur trade, possums spread across the motu. They strip the forest canopy kiwi depend on for shelter and food.

Rats colonised every corner

Ship rats and Norway rats arrived with European settlers. They raid kiwi nests — eating eggs and killing newly hatched chicks before they have a chance to run.

The stoat: kiwi’s deadliest predator

In mast years — when forests seed heavily — stoat populations explode. A single stoat can kill 95% of kiwi chicks in a nesting area within one season.

What this means

Every sighting logged is a contribution to kiwi survival.

The data shows that where communities engage, conservation follows — and wildlife responds.

The collection pipeline is working. Observations logged on iNaturalist NZ are verified by the community, reach Research Grade, and flow automatically into GBIF — where they sit alongside records from researchers, universities, and museum collections, all queryable through a single open API. Cross-referencing that sighting data against DOC’s completed pesticide operations and Trap.NZ community trapping projects reveals a partial but meaningful answer — and one genuine gap.

Where the community is responding — and it shows

Wellington has the highest predator density of any region and the highest number of community Trap.NZ projects (323). Kiwi are not naturally wild in Wellington — the GBIF sightings here almost certainly come from Zealandia, the fenced predator-free sanctuary in Karori. But the community trapping effort is producing real results beyond the fence line: kākā, once locally extinct in Wellington, have spread from Zealandia back into suburban neighbourhoods and are now a common sight across the city. The loop is closing — not for kiwi yet, but for the broader ecosystem that makes kiwi recovery possible. Northland tells a different story: the highest conservation-to-predator ratio of any region, and 1,107 kiwi sightings — genuine wild populations — to show for it.

One region stands out as a gap

Canterbury has the third-highest predator sighting density in the country, but only 14 DOC operations and 107 Trap.NZ projects — the second-lowest operations-to-predator ratio after Southland. Unlike Southland, this is not explained by low predator pressure. It has zero recorded kiwi sightings in the GBIF dataset. Historically, kiwi were present across much of the South Island including Canterbury — but conversion of the Canterbury Plains to farmland, combined with unchecked predation, drove them out over generations. With high predator density and limited conservation response, there is currently little realistic pathway for their return. That combination — a species that was once here, predators that are still here, and operations that largely aren’t — is the most concerning pattern in the dataset.

Where kiwi thrive, the data explains why

Southland has more kiwi sightings than any other region (2,417) and the fewest predator records — and the geography explains much of it. The region encompasses Stewart Island/Rakiura, estimated to hold around 20,000 kiwi and one of the last places in New Zealand where they live largely free from introduced predators, as well as the remote wilderness of Fiordland National Park, home to Piopiotahi (Milford Sound) and vast stretches of terrain so inaccessible that stoats, rats, and possums have never reached full density. These are places where isolation has done what conservation operations elsewhere have to achieve deliberately. Few interventions are needed because geography has already provided the buffer — a reminder that the best conservation outcome is one where the data shows nothing alarming. The question is whether that natural protection holds as climate change shifts mast years and predator ranges.

How it was built

Data · Python + REST API
A Python script calls the GBIF /occurrence/search endpoint, paginating through up to 300 verified records per species and writing the results to CSV.
Visualisation · Plotly
Six species are rendered as individual scattergeo traces. Plotly’s restyle method swaps trace visibility on each scroll step without re-rendering the full chart.
Scroll logic · Scrollama
Scrollama fires step-enter and step-exit events as the user scrolls. Each event maps to a predator layer, creating the layered reveal without any CSS animation.
