Field Notes · NZ Conservation

What the data says about
the species we're losing — and saving.

New Zealand's conservation record is genuinely mixed. Some species are recovering — the result of decades of sustained effort and a whaling moratorium that finally held. Others are disappearing faster than the trapping operations can keep up. These data stories try to show both sides honestly.

Sources: GBIF (Global Biodiversity Information Facility), iNaturalist NZ, DOC conservation operations data, Trap.NZ. All sightings are verified Research Grade citizen science records.

Marine wildlife · Humpback Whale · GBIF data

Not all native species are in decline

The humpback whale was hunted to near-extinction from New Zealand shores. 38 years after the international whaling moratorium, they're back — and the GBIF sighting data shows exactly how the recovery maps across the Pacific. One of conservation's genuine success stories, told through verified citizen science observations.

The same data pipeline that researchers use

Both pieces in Field Notes are built on GBIF — the Global Biodiversity Information Facility — which aggregates verified sightings from iNaturalist NZ, DOC, and museum collections into a freely queryable global database. Every sighting is Research Grade: verified by at least two independent observers before it's included.

That means these maps aren't illustrative — they're the same primary source data that DOC and conservation researchers work from. When a citizen scientist logs a kiwi sighting on iNaturalist NZ, it reaches GBIF automatically. This pipeline queries it directly.
