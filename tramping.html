Field Notes · Outdoor safety · NZ backcountry

The backcountry gap —
why foreign hikers die when locals don't.

International visitors account for around 13% of search and rescue callouts in New Zealand's backcountry. They account for close to half of all deaths. The gap isn't explained by bad luck — it's explained by a predictable chain of failures that a $25 device could help break.

The disparity

Two very different numbers

Before New Zealand's borders closed in 2020, international visitors made up roughly 12–14% of all search and rescue callouts in the backcountry. That's not a majority. It's not even a quarter. But in the decade ending 2017, 57 people died on New Zealand's tracks — and international tourists made up close to half of those deaths.

The gap is not about frequency. Tourists aren't getting into trouble more often than locals. When they do get into trouble, they are far more likely to die. That distinction matters for understanding what's actually going wrong — and what could fix it.

Why it happens

Three failures that keep repeating

Coroner's reports, DOC ranger accounts, and Mountain Safety Council research point to the same pattern across incidents involving overseas visitors. The environment isn't uniquely deadly — but it is unforgiving of three specific mistakes.

01
Wrong gear for the wrong conditions
DOC rangers have documented tourists at trailheads in skinny jeans, snowboarding boots, cashmere sweaters, and mesh-cut leggings. New Zealand's weather can shift from warm to dangerous in under an hour. Cotton and fashion gear become hypothermia risks.
02
Grading that doesn't translate
New Zealand's DOC track grades are based on fitness level, not technical difficulty. A "Tramping Track" signals nothing about river crossings, alpine exposure, or navigation skill. Visitors from countries with standardised alpine grading systems read this as easier than it is.
03
The Great Walks false ceiling
New Zealand's Great Walks are well-maintained, well-marked, and well-serviced. Completing them can lead visitors to believe they're ready for backcountry routes that require genuine navigation, river-crossing competence, and the ability to bivouac safely. They often aren't.

The risk-per-day for international visitors in New Zealand's national parks has been estimated at 4–6 times higher than for New Zealand residents doing the same trips. That multiplier isn't about the environment being different — it's about preparation being different.

Drowning at river crossings is particularly significant because it is often survivable with faster rescue response. A swollen river crossing gone wrong doesn't have to be fatal — but if no one knows where you are, it will be.

The fix

A $25 device that most tourists never hear about

A Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) doesn't prevent accidents. It compresses rescue response time from days to hours — and in exposure, drowning survival, and hypothermia scenarios, that compression is often the difference between survival and a fatality statistic.

New Zealand already has the infrastructure. The problem is delivery.

What already exists

The rental network is there

100K+ PLBs in circulation in New Zealand
92 rental outlets nationwide
550 units available for hire through Southland Locator Beacons alone
$15–$30 per trip — less than a restaurant meal

What's missing

The moment of friction

DOC recommends beacons. That's it. There is no mandatory check at visitor centre check-in for backcountry tracks. No PLB rental prompt when purchasing Great Walk tickets online — DOC's primary touchpoint with international visitors. No multilingual beacon information at the trailheads where tourists make their last decision before going in.

The infrastructure exists. The moment of intervention doesn't.

The cost

What the current system spends

$42.1M Government SAR spend per year (2021–22), covering operations, training, and equipment
~$150K Cost of a single complex rescue operation — one callout can exceed annual PLB rental revenue
$0 Charged to trampers for rescue in New Zealand — one of few countries where SAR is entirely taxpayer-funded

The reputational cost is harder to quantify but visible. In 2018, South China Morning Post ran the headline: "New Zealand asks locals to help save foreign hikers' lives after spate of accidents and incidents." New Zealand markets itself as a world-class wilderness destination. The deaths of overseas visitors in preventable circumstances — no beacon, wrong gear, ignored weather warnings — are the kind of story that travels.

What would actually change things

Four things that don't require new infrastructure

None of these proposals require building anything new. They require changing defaults — shifting the burden from opt-in awareness to opt-out decision.

01

Bundle a PLB prompt into every Great Walk booking confirmation

DOC already operates the booking platform. A single additional screen — "Would you like to hire a personal locator beacon for your trip? $25. Pick up at the visitor centre." — would reach the exact population most likely to continue into harder terrain afterward. Opt-out, not opt-in.

02

Add an international difficulty equivalent to track grades

New Zealand's five-grade system is locally understood but opaque to visitors. A secondary label — cross-referenced with European Alpine grading or the Yosemite Decimal System — would help experienced overseas hikers calibrate risk accurately. A "Tramping Track" that involves unguided river crossings deserves a different signal than one that doesn't.

03

Multilingual safety information at the 20 highest-incident trailheads

Current DOC safety signage at backcountry trailheads is English-only. International visitors who die on NZ tracks include significant proportions of Mandarin, Korean, German, and French speakers. Safety information in the languages of the people actually using the tracks is a low-cost, high-signal change.

04

Real-time river hazard alerts for registered hikers

Drowning accounts for 26% of tramping deaths, most at river crossings that were safe when the hike began. Many flood events are predictable 12–24 hours ahead. An SMS or app alert system for hikers who have registered on high-risk routes is technically straightforward and could prevent the most common single cause of tramping fatalities.

The question this data raises isn't whether New Zealand has a missing trampers problem. It doesn't — not in volume terms. The question is why the gap between callout rate and mortality rate is so large for one specific group of people, and whether the answer to that is something New Zealand has chosen to do nothing about.
