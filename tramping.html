Field Notes · Outdoor safety · NZ backcountry

The backcountry gap —
why foreign hikers die when locals don't.

International visitors account for around 13% of search and rescue callouts in New Zealand's backcountry. They account for close to half of all deaths. The gap isn't explained by bad luck — it's explained by a predictable chain of failures that a $25 device could help break.

The disparity

Two very different numbers

Before New Zealand's borders closed in 2020, international visitors made up roughly 12–14% of all search and rescue callouts in the backcountry. That's not a majority. It's not even a quarter. But in the decade ending 2017, 57 people died on New Zealand's tracks — and international tourists made up close to half of those deaths.

International visitors make up roughly 40% of all trampers in New Zealand. If callouts and deaths tracked participation, you'd expect tourists in both columns at around 40%. They're at 13% in one and nearly 50% in the other. The gap isn't about frequency — tourists aren't getting into trouble more often than locals. It's about what happens when they do. And increasingly, the answer appears to be: nothing, until someone finds them.

Why it happens

Three failures that keep repeating

Coroner's reports, DOC ranger accounts, and Mountain Safety Council research point to the same pattern across incidents involving overseas visitors. The environment isn't uniquely deadly — but it is unforgiving of three specific mistakes.

01
Wrong gear for the wrong conditions
DOC rangers have documented tourists at trailheads in skinny jeans, snowboarding boots, cashmere sweaters, and mesh-cut leggings. New Zealand's weather can shift from warm to dangerous in under an hour. Cotton and fashion gear become hypothermia risks.
02
Grading that doesn't translate
New Zealand's DOC track grades are based on fitness level, not technical difficulty. A "Tramping Track" signals nothing about river crossings, alpine exposure, or navigation skill. Visitors from countries with standardised alpine grading systems read this as easier than it is.
03
The Great Walks false ceiling
New Zealand's Great Walks are well-maintained, well-marked, and well-serviced. Completing them can lead visitors to believe they're ready for backcountry routes that require genuine navigation, river-crossing competence, and the ability to bivouac safely. They often aren't.

Working from the participation data, international visitors die at roughly 1.5 times the rate of locals per tramping trip. That gap is real but modest — the environment isn't dramatically more dangerous for tourists. What is dramatically different is what happens after something goes wrong.

Drowning at river crossings is particularly significant because it is often survivable with faster rescue response. A swollen river crossing gone wrong doesn't have to be fatal — but if no one knows where you are, it will be.

Case study · Nelson Lakes · 2014

Christian Prehn — 19 years old, never found

He was a German backpacker on a gap year. Well-equipped, experienced enough to attempt the Travers-Sabine Circuit alone. He went missing on 25 February 2014 and his body was never recovered. His case sits at the intersection of everything this story is about: a solo international visitor, no PLB, no trip contact, and a three-day gap before anyone raised the alarm — closed only because a stranger found his abandoned pack by chance.

The Travers-Sabine Circuit

An 80km alpine loop in Nelson Lakes National Park

The Travers-Sabine is one of New Zealand's classic multi-day circuits — crossing Travers Saddle at 1,787m, through steep, remote alpine terrain. It is not a Great Walk. There are no rangers, no phone signal, and no huts with emergency comms above the valley floor.

Christian Prehn, 19, from Germany, had been in New Zealand since October 2013. By February 2014 he was experienced enough to plan it solo.

Green line: confirmed route. Gold dashed: presumed summit attempt. No cell coverage exists in the upper Travers Valley or at Travers Saddle.

23 February 2014

He parks his car at St Arnaud and begins

He left his car in the St Arnaud village carpark with provisions for several days and a planned return date of 1 March. He did not file a trip intention. No one was waiting for him to return.

Filing a trip intention means leaving your route, return date, and an emergency contact with someone who will call Police if you don't check in. It costs nothing and takes five minutes. Most international visitors don't know it exists.

24–25 February 2014

Upper Travers Hut — the last confirmed rest stop

He spent a night at Upper Travers Hut, the highest hut on the Travers side before the saddle. Two other trampers on the circuit encountered him here. They later confirmed he seemed well — experienced, properly equipped, in good spirits.

They were the last people to see him alive at the hut. Their account became crucial to the timeline searchers later tried to reconstruct.

25 February 2014, ~11:30am

Below Travers Saddle — the last confirmed sighting

The same two trampers encountered him again on the track below Travers Saddle, heading upward. He was described as "standing and looking up at the black peak of Mt Travers." They talked about attempting the summit.

He said he would leave his pack at the saddle and go up light. The two trampers continued on the circuit. That was the last confirmed sighting.

"Standing and looking up at the black peak of Mt Travers." — Wilderness Magazine account of the final sighting

25 February 2014 — presumed

Mt Travers — 2,338 metres

Mt Travers is the second-highest peak in Nelson Lakes National Park. The terrain is described as "treacherously steep," consisting of rock that readily fractures. There is no marked route to the summit.

What happened on the mountain is not known. His body was never recovered. Not a footprint. Not a food wrapper. The coroner ruled accidental death.

No PLB. No cell coverage. No trip intention. If he fell, there was no mechanism in place for anyone to know where to look — or even that he needed help.

1 March 2014 — three days later

A stranger finds his pack. The alarm is raised.

A couple walking the circuit came across his grey Lowe Alpine pack at Travers Saddle — full of gear, personal items, and food. They notified DOC staff. A search began the following day.

The search was halted on 6 March by snow and -10°C windchill. A second community-funded search was run in 2015, paid for by €5,400 raised at a music school concert in Germany. No trace was ever found.

He wasn't reported missing because anyone who knew him raised the alarm. He was reported missing because a stranger found his belongings. Without that chance encounter, the alert could have come days later — or not at all.

The deeper question

Why aren't tourists calling for rescue?

International visitors make up roughly 40% of all trampers in New Zealand — about 600,000 of the 1.5 million people who tramped in 2017. If everything were proportional, you'd expect around 40% of SAR callouts to involve tourists. The actual figure is 13%.

That gap isn't explained by tourists being more careful. The death rate tells you they're not. It's explained by tourists being unable to call.

A SAR callout in New Zealand's backcountry requires one of three things: a PLB, which activates a satellite signal without any cell coverage; cellphone reception, which doesn't exist on most backcountry tracks; or a trip intention filed with someone back home who will call police if you don't return by a set time. Locals use all three. Most tourists arrive without any of them — no beacon, no NZ SIM, and no one back home who knows exactly where they are or when to raise the alarm.

When a local gets into trouble, a callout happens. When a tourist gets into the same trouble, often nothing happens — until someone finds them. The 13% figure isn't evidence that tourists are safe. It's evidence that their incidents are dying silently.

The fix

A $25 device that most tourists never hear about

A Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) doesn't prevent accidents. But for a tourist with no local SIM and no trip intention lodged, it is the only mechanism that exists to summon help from remote terrain. Without one, they are entirely dependent on someone eventually noticing they haven't come back — and in a country where solo tourists move between hostels on flexible itineraries, that can take days.

New Zealand already has the infrastructure. The problem is delivery.

What already exists

The rental network is there

100K+ PLBs in circulation in New Zealand
92 rental outlets nationwide
550 units available for hire through Southland Locator Beacons alone
$15–$30 per trip — less than a restaurant meal

What's missing

The moment of friction

DOC recommends beacons. That's it. There is no mandatory check at visitor centre check-in for backcountry tracks. No PLB rental prompt when purchasing Great Walk tickets online — DOC's primary touchpoint with international visitors. No multilingual beacon information at the trailheads where tourists make their last decision before going in.

The infrastructure exists. The moment of intervention doesn't.

The cost

What the current system spends

$42.1M Government SAR spend per year (2021–22), covering operations, training, and equipment
$11.7M ACC spent in 2024 helping people recover from tramping injuries — 9,009 claims
~$150K Cost of a single complex rescue operation — one callout can exceed annual PLB rental revenue
$0 Charged to trampers for rescue in New Zealand — one of few countries where SAR is entirely taxpayer-funded

The reputational cost is harder to quantify but visible. In 2018, South China Morning Post ran the headline: "New Zealand asks locals to help save foreign hikers' lives after spate of accidents and incidents." New Zealand markets itself as a world-class wilderness destination. The deaths of overseas visitors in preventable circumstances — no beacon, wrong gear, ignored weather warnings — are the kind of story that travels.

What would actually change things

Four things that don't require new infrastructure

None of these proposals require building anything new. They require changing defaults — shifting the burden from opt-in awareness to opt-out decision.

01

Bundle a PLB prompt into every Great Walk booking confirmation

DOC already operates the booking platform. A single additional screen — "Would you like to hire a personal locator beacon for your trip? $25. Pick up at the visitor centre." — would reach the exact population most likely to continue into harder terrain afterward. Opt-out, not opt-in.

02

Add an international difficulty equivalent to track grades

New Zealand's five-grade system is locally understood but opaque to visitors. A secondary label — cross-referenced with European Alpine grading or the Yosemite Decimal System — would help experienced overseas hikers calibrate risk accurately. A "Tramping Track" that involves unguided river crossings deserves a different signal than one that doesn't.

03

Multilingual safety information at the 20 highest-incident trailheads

Current DOC safety signage at backcountry trailheads is English-only. International visitors who die on NZ tracks include significant proportions of Mandarin, Korean, German, and French speakers. Safety information in the languages of the people actually using the tracks is a low-cost, high-signal change.

04

Real-time river hazard alerts for registered hikers

Drowning accounts for 26% of tramping deaths, most at river crossings that were safe when the hike began. Many flood events are predictable 12–24 hours ahead. An SMS or app alert system for hikers who have registered on high-risk routes is technically straightforward and could prevent the most common single cause of tramping fatalities.

The question this data raises isn't whether New Zealand has a missing trampers problem. It doesn't — not in volume terms. The question is why the gap between callout rate and mortality rate is so large for one specific group of people, and whether the answer to that is something New Zealand has chosen to do nothing about.

A note on data vintage
The 13% callout / ~50% death split, and the ~40% international tramper participation figure, come from MSC research covering the decade to June 2017. New Zealand's borders closed in March 2020 and international visitor numbers only recovered from 2022 onward — which means no equivalent post-pandemic study yet exists for the tourist-specific figures. The ACC injury data (9,009 claims, $11.7M) is current to 2024. Overall SAR incident volumes have remained broadly stable since 2018, though the proportion of fatal incidents has increased.
