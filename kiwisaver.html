Membership

Who is in KiwiSaver?

The 25–34 and 35–44 age bands are the largest cohorts — exactly the groups most likely to be saving for a first home or peak earning years. Membership in the 65+ bracket is growing fastest as the scheme matures and older members choose to stay enrolled. The under-17 cohort is gradually shrinking as fewer children are enrolled by parents.

On contribution rates, 3% dominates — the legal minimum — with over 1.16 million members contributing at that level. The 6% rate is the only one growing; 4% and 8% are declining. Nearly 1.6 million members made no contribution at all in 2025, a figure that includes children, retirees, those on savings suspension, and overseas members.

First home buyers

KiwiSaver as a path to home ownership

First home withdrawals peaked in 2021 — the height of the housing boom — when 54,520 members used KiwiSaver to help buy their first home, withdrawing $1.6 billion. As the market cooled and interest rates rose, withdrawals dipped. They are now recovering: 43,600 people made a first home withdrawal in 2025, withdrawing a record $1.86 billion. The average first home withdrawal is approximately $43,000 — a meaningful contribution to a deposit, but still a fraction of what most regions require.

Policy update · 1 April 2026

Two changes. One clear shift of cost.

From 1 April 2026, employers must contribute a minimum of 3.5% of an employee's salary into KiwiSaver — up from 3%, and now applying to 16 and 17 year olds for the first time. Simultaneously, the government has halved its own member tax credit: the maximum annual government contribution drops from $521.43 to $260.72. The government saves approximately $417 million per year — and passes the obligation to employers.

The net effect depends heavily on what you earn. The employer gain (a fixed percentage of salary) grows with income, while the government credit loss ($260.71) is the same for almost everyone who contributes enough. The break-even point is ~$74,500 — earners below that threshold are net worse off from the combined changes.

A note of caution

Financial hardship withdrawals are rising

For the first time in KiwiSaver's history, the number of members withdrawing under financial hardship exceeded the number withdrawing for a first home purchase in 2025 — 45,870 versus 43,600.

The trend has been building for years. Hardship withdrawals have risen in every single year since 2012, accelerating sharply from 2023 as cost-of-living pressures mounted. The average hardship withdrawal is around $10,000 — a fraction of the first home average, reflecting the crisis-level circumstances that trigger it.

The implication is sobering: KiwiSaver is increasingly being drawn upon not as a path to home ownership, but as a last financial resort. For many New Zealanders, the savings intended for retirement or a first home are being consumed by the pressures of today.

This data should be read alongside the housing deprivation and rental figures in the main story. The people most likely to be withdrawing under hardship are the same people least likely to ever reach the deposit threshold for home ownership.
