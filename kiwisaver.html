Deep Dive · KiwiSaver Analysis

Inside KiwiSaver —
eighteen years of data

KiwiSaver launched on 1 July 2007 under Finance Minister Michael Cullen — a deliberate response to New Zealand’s chronically low household savings rate and growing anxiety about the long-term cost of NZ Super. The idea was straightforward: auto-enrol workers, attach savings to employment, and let compounding do the rest.

What followed was messier. The scheme survived a change of government in 2008 — despite National’s early opposition — but the employer contribution rate stalled at 2% for six years, and the government’s own contribution was cut twice. Today, 3.4 million New Zealanders are enrolled, $123 billion sits in KiwiSaver funds, and the scheme faces a defining tension: it is simultaneously a retirement savings vehicle, a first home deposit tool, and — increasingly — a financial hardship lifeline.

Membership

Who is in KiwiSaver?

The 25–34 and 35–44 age bands are the largest cohorts — exactly the groups most likely to be saving for a first home or peak earning years. Membership in the 65+ bracket is growing fastest as the scheme matures and older members choose to stay enrolled. The under-17 cohort is gradually shrinking as fewer children are enrolled by parents.

On contribution rates, 3% dominates — the legal minimum — with over 1.16 million members contributing at that level. The 6% rate is the only one growing; 4% and 8% are declining. Nearly 1.6 million members made no contribution at all in 2025, a figure that includes children, retirees, those on savings suspension, and overseas members.

First home buyers

KiwiSaver as a path to home ownership

First home withdrawals peaked in 2021 — the height of the housing boom — when 54,520 members used KiwiSaver to help buy their first home, withdrawing $1.6 billion. As the market cooled and interest rates rose, withdrawals dipped. They are now recovering: 43,600 people made a first home withdrawal in 2025, withdrawing a record $1.86 billion. The average first home withdrawal is approximately $43,000 — a meaningful contribution to a deposit, but still a fraction of what most regions require.

A note of caution

Financial hardship withdrawals are rising

For the first time in KiwiSaver’s history, the number of members withdrawing under financial hardship exceeded the number withdrawing for a first home purchase in 2025 — 45,870 versus 43,600.

The trend has been building for years. Hardship withdrawals have risen in every single year since 2012, accelerating sharply from 2023 as cost-of-living pressures mounted. The average hardship withdrawal is around $10,000 — a fraction of the first home average, reflecting the crisis-level circumstances that trigger it.

The implication is sobering: KiwiSaver is increasingly being drawn upon not as a path to home ownership, but as a last financial resort. For many New Zealanders, the savings intended for retirement or a first home are being consumed by the pressures of today.

The compounding cost

What a $10,000 hardship withdrawal really costs

Every dollar withdrawn under hardship is a dollar that stops compounding. At a conservative 6% annual return — typical for a balanced KiwiSaver fund after fees — a $10,000 withdrawal at age 30 doesn’t just cost $10,000. It costs the $77,000 that money would have become by retirement. For a 25 year old, that same withdrawal costs over $100,000. The hardship withdrawal scheme exists for good reason, but the long-term retirement penalty is rarely visible to the people making the decision under financial pressure.

The compounding cost of financial precarity

Economic precarity doesn’t end when the immediate crisis passes — it compounds

The 45,870 people who withdrew under hardship in 2025 were not making a financial planning error. They were paying rent. Covering groceries. Keeping the power on. The cost of living crisis doesn’t ask permission before it raids tomorrow’s retirement savings — and when it does, the cost doesn’t end when the crisis passes. It compounds.

The Crown contributed roughly 8% of every dollar that went into KiwiSaver — around $38 million of the $471 million withdrawn under hardship in 2025 was taxpayer money. It went in as a long-term investment in retirement security. It came out to cover this week’s expenses. That is the cost of the cost of living crisis made visible: public money spent twice, on the same person, because the underlying pressure was never addressed.

The longer-term fiscal consequence is larger still. NZ Super costs $18.5 billion a year — roughly 5% of GDP — and was designed as a foundation, not a full income. KiwiSaver was meant to be the structure built on top of it. When hardship withdrawals hollow out that structure for the people least likely to rebuild it, the Crown is left carrying a retirement income system under greater strain than it should be. A society that allows poverty to persist pays for it twice: once in the crisis, and again in retirement.

Policy update · Two dates. One direction.

Two changes. One clear shift of cost.

The two changes don’t arrive together. The government halved its member tax credit on 1 July 2025. The employer rate increase — from 3% to 3.5% — doesn’t kick in until 1 April 2026. In the nine months between, members absorb the full loss with nothing to offset it. The government saves ~$313 million in that window. KiwiSavers collectively lose ~$294 million.

From April 2026, the employer increase almost — but not quite — covers the credit loss for most people. The median earner on $69,000 is still $19 worse off per year after both changes take effect.

Trans-Tasman comparison

Australia’s super system puts KiwiSaver in perspective

Both countries have mandatory workplace savings schemes, but the similarities end there. Australia’s Superannuation Guarantee has been compulsory since 1992 and is currently set at 12% — more than three times KiwiSaver’s employer rate. The result is a retirement savings system that dwarfs KiwiSaver in every measurable way. New Zealand’s opt-out system has achieved strong uptake, but the contribution gap compounds over a working lifetime into a stark difference in retirement outcomes.

Context

Is this comparison fair?

✓
The case for comparison
  • Closest peers. New Zealand and Australia are each other’s most comparable counterparts — both OECD members, English-speaking Pacific economies with similar labour markets and living standards.
  • Shared conceptions of retirement. Both countries share broadly the same cultural expectations of what a comfortable retirement looks like — the age at which people stop working, the lifestyle they expect to maintain, and the role of home ownership as a wealth foundation.
  • Designed in parallel. KiwiSaver was explicitly modelled with Australia’s system in mind. Policymakers have long used Australian superannuation as both an inspiration and a benchmark when debating contribution rate increases.
  • Free movement of people. Under the Trans-Tasman Travel Arrangement, New Zealanders can and do live and work in Australia — and vice versa. Retirement savings accumulated in one country directly affects what people have when they retire in the other.
!
The limits of comparison
  • Scale and wage base. Australia has five times New Zealand’s population, significantly higher median wages, and a far larger tax base. Higher salaries mean employer contributions compound faster — KiwiSaver’s percentage gap looks even wider in dollar terms.
  • Structural economic advantages. Australia benefits from vast natural resources, large-scale mining and energy exports, deep capital markets, and a substantial financial services and technology sector. These underpin higher wages and stronger investment returns in super funds.
  • Capital flows out of New Zealand. Many of New Zealand’s largest employers — the major banks, supermarkets, and insurers — are Australian-owned. Profits flow across the Tasman rather than being reinvested domestically, which constrains the wage growth and business investment that would otherwise feed into KiwiSaver balances.
  • Thirty years of compounding. Australia’s system became mandatory in 1992 — KiwiSaver launched in 2007. Much of the gap in total assets reflects fifteen extra years of compounding at higher rates. New Zealand is not behind in ambition; it is behind in time.

The comparison is most useful not as a verdict but as a direction. Australia shows what a mature, high-contribution retirement system can produce. Whether New Zealand can — or should — close that gap depends on political will, wage growth, and what New Zealanders decide retirement is for.

Citizens filling the gap

Where policy falls short, organisations are stepping in

The hardship data tells one story. But alongside it, a quieter one: a growing ecosystem of not-for-profits, community lenders, and new-model financial platforms working on the same problem from different angles. Some are lowering the cost of saving. Some are providing alternatives to raiding KiwiSaver. Some are simply making sure people understand their options before it’s too late. None of them can replace structural policy change — but together they represent a meaningful shift in who takes responsibility for financial wellbeing in New Zealand.

Not-for-profit KiwiSaver

Simplicity

Founded in 2016 on a single premise: KiwiSaver fees are too high and the people who benefit most from lowering them are the people who can least afford not to. Simplicity is New Zealand’s only not-for-profit KiwiSaver provider, charging 0.24% — against an industry average of 1.05%. On a $50,000 balance, that’s $388 saved every year, compounding. It has cut fees seven times since launch. Fifteen percent of all management fees go directly to New Zealand charities — $13.3 million donated to date.

Free financial mentoring

FinCap · MoneyTalks

A network of over 700 free financial mentors operating across 180 services nationwide. FinCap helped 69,807 whānau in 2023 — a 40.8% increase on the year before. That increase isn’t growth for its own sake; it’s a measure of how many more people are under financial pressure. The median presenting debt is $14,096. MoneyTalks (0800 345 123) is a free helpline that connects people to a mentor before a KiwiSaver hardship withdrawal starts to look like the only option.

The case for optimism

KiwiSaver is working — and before it,
there was almost nothing

Before KiwiSaver, New Zealand had no meaningful workplace savings infrastructure. Household savings rates were among the lowest in the OECD — negative in some years — and the vast majority of New Zealanders arrived at retirement with NZ Super as their only income. That universal pension, worth around 60% of the average wage, was designed as a floor. For most people, it became the ceiling.

KiwiSaver changed that. Launched in 2007 as a voluntary opt-out scheme, it enrolled 3.4 million New Zealanders — 63.5% of the entire population — without compulsion. The scheme has survived three changes of government, two contribution rate cuts, and the worst cost-of-living crisis in a generation. It is still standing, still growing, and still putting money away for people who would otherwise have saved nothing.

The trajectory also matters. The employer rate rises to 3.5% in April 2026 and 4% in 2028. For a 25-year-old on the median wage today, that legislative shift compounds into tens of thousands of extra dollars by retirement. The gap with Australia is real — but it is narrowing, and the direction is set.

What this means

Six things the data is telling policymakers

01
KiwiSaver needs to be re-evaluated as a social safety net, not just a retirement vehicle.Hardship withdrawals now exceed first home withdrawals for the first time. The scheme is being used as a last resort by tens of thousands of New Zealanders. Policy designed around retirement and home ownership is not built for this reality — access rules, contribution design, and hardship criteria all need to reflect both purposes.
02
The April 2026 changes disproportionately hurt lower earners and should be offset.The break-even salary is ~$74,500 — above the median. Anyone earning less is net worse off from the combined employer increase and tax credit cut. An income-indexed member tax credit, rather than a flat cut, would protect the people most reliant on the government contribution.
03
Auto-escalation would shift the contribution rate distribution without reducing enrolment.Over 1.16 million members are contributing at exactly 3% — the minimum. Schemes in the UK and US have demonstrated that automatically increasing contributions by a small percentage each year, unless actively opted out, shifts this distribution significantly while maintaining enrolment rates. New Zealand has not yet implemented this.
04
Closing the Trans-Tasman gap has a clear lever — and it is already in motion.Australia’s 12% rate was not legislated overnight — it rose incrementally from 3% over thirty years. New Zealand’s rate is now moving in the same direction. The scheduled increase to 4% in 2028 matters less for its immediate dollar impact than for the trajectory it sets. The question is whether future governments maintain the direction.
05
Organisations filling the gap point to where structural policy has failed.The growth of FinCap, Ngā Tāngata, Good Shepherd, and others is not a success story — it is a signal. A 40% year-on-year increase in demand for free financial mentoring reflects the scale of financial stress in New Zealand households. These organisations are doing vital work, but their growth is a measure of a problem, not a solution to it.
06
The net effect of both changes is nearly zero — which raises the question of what the policy actually achieved.For the median earner, cutting the government credit and raising the employer rate produces an annual net change of −$19. One step back, one step forward. The outcome for members is broadly unchanged — but the funding source has shifted permanently from the government to employers. Whether that rebalancing is the right long-term direction is a legitimate debate. What the data makes clear is that it has not been presented as one.
