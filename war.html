The Age of Impunity — war begets war

2024 recorded more active armed conflicts than at any point since records began in 1946. This is not a coincidence. As Western economic power declines, the cost of starting a war falls. As democracies normalise military force, others follow their lead. This is the evidence.

UCDP · State-based armed conflicts

The trend — 2000 to 2024

More armed conflicts are active today than at any point since records began in 1946

The Uppsala Conflict Data Program — the gold standard for conflict tracking — recorded 61 state-based armed conflicts in 2024, up from 59 in 2023. That is the highest number since UCDP began collecting data in 1946. Eleven reached the threshold of "war" — 1,000+ deaths in a year — the most since 2016. The number has risen in 16 of the last 20 years. This is not a post-pandemic blip. It is a structural shift.

61 active conflicts · 2024 record

2005–2020: the baseline

The 15 years before 2020 averaged 43 active conflicts per year

Between 2005 and 2020, the world averaged roughly 43 active state-based armed conflicts per year. This period included Iraq, Afghanistan, Syria, Libya, and Yemen — already a significant toll. But relative to what came after, it was the floor. The international rules-based order — however imperfect — was still functioning as a deterrent.

2020–2024: the surge

From 2020, conflict numbers began accelerating sharply — driven by new wars, not just old ones

Russia's full-scale Ukraine invasion (2022), the resumption of war in Sudan (2023), the eruption of fighting in Gaza (2023), and the deepening of Myanmar's civil war drove the number from 56 in 2020 to 61 in 2024 — four years, four new major conflicts. Thesis 2

The Power Shift — the cost of aggression is falling

Western power has traditionally deterred aggression through two mechanisms. The first is economic: sanctions, trade restrictions, financial isolation. The second is military: the credible threat that the United States, or a Western coalition, will intervene. Both are weakening simultaneously — and it is the combination that defines this moment.

Declining G7 economic weight means sanctions can be absorbed or routed around. Overstretched military commitments, domestic political fractures, and the lessons of Afghanistan mean that for a growing category of conflicts, the military threat simply isn't credible. Smaller nations and non-state actors are doing this maths explicitly. The honest answer to "will the West intervene?" is, for most conflicts in the world today, probably not. That calculation is what the age of impunity is built on.

The mechanism is straightforward. Economic coercion works when the coercing bloc has no substitute. When Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022, the West imposed the most sweeping sanctions package in history. They slowed Russian growth — but did not stop the war, and did not cause the economic collapse that was predicted. China absorbed Russian energy exports. India bought discounted Russian oil. The SWIFT alternative CIPS processed China-Russia transactions. Iran, sanctioned for decades, continued its proxy wars throughout the Middle East.

The lesson was not lost. Every potential aggressor watched and noted: sanctions hurt, but they do not stop a war. They are a cost to be managed — not a veto on military action.

Part 2 of the power shift

The intervention calculation — who can now act without consequence

Before starting a war, every government and armed group runs a version of the same calculation: what will the West do? For most of the post-Cold War period, that question carried real uncertainty. The Gulf War showed the US would act. Kosovo, Bosnia, Libya — the West would sometimes intervene even without direct strategic interest. That uncertainty was itself a deterrent. It is no longer uncertain. A generation of evidence has made the answer to "will the US intervene?" largely predictable — and the conditions under which the answer is "no" now cover most of the world.

What goes into the calculation

Is the US already in another major conflict?
Ukraine support, Middle East strikes, and Taiwan deterrence are simultaneously consuming US military bandwidth, political capital, and public appetite for engagement. Each active commitment reduces the credible threat of opening a new one.
Is there domestic political will?
Post-Afghanistan, post-Iraq, and in the Trump era of explicit non-interventionism, US domestic support for military action in strategically peripheral countries is near zero. Authoritarian leaders read American polling data.
Is there a nuclear weapon or a nuclear patron?
Nuclear weapons are the most reliable guarantor of non-intervention. North Korea is untouchable. Russia cannot be directly engaged. Iran's nuclear threshold deters all but targeted strikes. A Chinese security relationship performs the same function for non-nuclear states.
Is the target covered by a formal US alliance?
NATO Article 5 provides a credible guarantee. Outside that formal architecture — which excludes most of the world — the guarantee is political, not legal, and subject to revision. Ukraine learned this in 2022.

The no-credible-cost zone

Countries or actors that score "no" on all the deterrence variables operate in near-impunity. The profile is consistent:

Not in NATO or a formal US alliance — the written guarantee doesn't apply
Not strategically vital to Western interests — no semiconductor fabs, no major oil routes, no trade dependency
Victims are non-European — the political pressure for Western intervention is demonstrably lower
Has a Russian or Chinese patron — Security Council veto blocks any UN authorisation

Countries in this zone today: Sudan, Myanmar, Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso, DRC, Haiti, Ethiopia, Somalia, Yemen. Combined population: over 400 million people. Current Western military intervention in any of them: zero.

The moments that sharpened the calculation — watched by every government in the world

2013 — Syria
Obama drew a "red line" on chemical weapons use. Assad crossed it. No US military action followed. Lesson filed: stated commitments from Washington are conditional and reversible.
2014 — Ukraine/Crimea
Russia annexed Crimea. The Budapest Memorandum — signed by the US, UK, and Russia — had guaranteed Ukraine's territorial integrity. Western response: sanctions. Lesson: written guarantees to non-NATO states are not military guarantees.
2021 — Afghanistan
The US withdrew after 20 years. The government it built collapsed in 11 days. Lesson: Western commitment is finite. Patient adversaries — states and non-state actors alike — can outlast it by simply waiting.
2022 — Ukraine
Russia invaded a European country for the first time since WWII. NATO did not send troops. The West armed Ukraine but explicitly ruled out direct military engagement. Lesson: even full-scale invasion of a Western partner does not trigger US boots on the ground.

"The Sahel juntas, Myanmar's generals, Sudan's RSF, and Hamas all ran the same calculation — and got the same answer. The West will issue statements. It may impose sanctions. It will not send soldiers. Once that answer is clear, the last meaningful deterrent is gone."

The evidence on the ground

The vacuum effect — where Western influence has already collapsed

The power shift is not an abstract future measured in GDP percentages. It has already produced a concrete, observable result: a wave of military coups across West Africa's Sahel, each expelling Western forces and inviting Russian mercenaries in — while Sudan burns and Western donors cut their aid budgets to fund priorities closer to home. Ukraine and Gaza are not merely humanitarian crises. They are consuming the diplomatic capital, military resources, and financial bandwidth that once underwrote Western deterrence everywhere else.

West Africa · 2020–2025

Six coups in five years — and France left with nothing

France had maintained a military presence in the Sahel for a decade — Operation Barkhane, thousands of troops, the logic of a secular democratic ally holding the line against jihadist expansion. Between 2020 and 2023, that entire architecture collapsed. Not through military defeat, but through a cascade of coups that each expelled Western forces and turned to Russia instead.

When the Sudan war began in April 2023, Western governments issued statements. They funded aid. They launched mediation talks in Jeddah — led by the US, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia — that produced nothing. Two years later, the response has not scaled to match the catastrophe. It has shrunk.

Afghanistan · 2001–2021

$2.3 trillion. Twenty years. Eleven days to collapse.

In August 2021, the United States completed its withdrawal from Afghanistan after twenty years of military presence, $2.3 trillion in spending, and the deaths of 241,000 people. The Afghan government the West had spent two decades building collapsed in eleven days. The Taliban — which had simply waited — walked into Kabul on August 15.

The Taliban did not defeat the West militarily. They outlasted it. The lesson — widely absorbed — is that Western security commitments are time-limited. Patient adversaries, insurgencies, and authoritarian governments watching from the outside updated their models: if you can survive long enough, America leaves.

What followed the withdrawal confirmed the analysis. Within months, the Taliban reversed every social and political gain of the previous two decades. Girls were banned first from secondary school, then from primary school — the most extreme restrictions on female education anywhere in the world. The economy contracted by over 30%. By late 2021, an estimated 97% of Afghans had fallen below the poverty line. The UN declared the largest humanitarian emergency on earth — briefly, before Sudan overtook it. And now Afghanistan is at war with Pakistan, its nuclear-armed neighbour, in a conflict that began with Pakistani airstrikes in October 2025.

Thesis 3

Democracy's Paradox — the precedents being set

The liberal argument against war rests on a foundational claim: that democracies are more restrained. That international law, public accountability, and shared values prevent the arbitrary use of military force. The last decade has severely tested that claim. The United States and Israel — the two most prominent Western military powers — have established a set of precedents that every authoritarian government is watching, and some are already citing.

This is not a moral judgement. It is a strategic observation. Norms are set by the most powerful actors' behaviour — not by their stated principles. When powerful democracies bypass international law, they do not just break a rule. They signal to everyone else that the rule is optional.

"The United States threatens to take Greenland from a NATO ally. Israel strikes Iran directly. Both cite security necessity. Every authoritarian leader in the world files this away."

Strategic logic · March 2025
2003
United States · Iraq
Invasion of Iraq — no UN mandate, no WMDs found
The US-led coalition invaded Iraq without a UN Security Council mandate, citing intelligence that proved false. The invasion killed an estimated 200,000+ civilians and destabilised an entire region. No legal accountability followed. The precedent: a permanent Security Council member can wage a war of choice — intelligence fabricated or not — and face no institutional consequence.
Precedent: wars of choice are permissible for the powerful
2025
United States · Venezuela
US government openly pursues regime decapitation — bounties, deportation threats, military options on the table
In early 2025, the Trump administration escalated its Venezuela posture from sanctions to explicit regime-change strategy. The Justice Department maintained a $25 million bounty on President Maduro for narco-terrorism charges. The administration invoked the Alien Enemies Act — a wartime statute last used in 1942 — to deport Venezuelan nationals to El Salvador's maximum-security Cecot prison without individual hearings, citing a gang designated as a foreign terrorist organisation. Senior officials publicly discussed military intervention options. This was not the language of diplomacy — it was the vocabulary of forcible government removal, deployed by a democracy against a civilian-led state it disfavoured. Beijing's leadership noted that Washington had just normalised the concept of targeting a foreign government's survival as a policy instrument. The question for Taiwan is straightforward: if the US can openly pursue regime decapitation in its own hemisphere, what constrains a regional power doing the same in theirs?
Precedent: forcible regime change — including leadership targeting — is a legitimate instrument of state policy
2023
Israel · Gaza
Gaza — 47,000+ killed, 2.1 million displaced, famine declared
Following the Hamas attacks of October 7, Israel launched a military campaign in Gaza that has killed over 47,000 people — more than two-thirds civilians according to Gaza health ministry figures — and displaced the entire population of the territory. The International Court of Justice issued provisional measures calling for Israel to prevent acts of genocide. The campaign continued. The US vetoed UN Security Council ceasefire resolutions multiple times. The ICJ measures were largely ignored.
Precedent: ICJ orders and UN resolutions are optional for US allies
2024
Israel · Lebanon
Full-scale invasion of Lebanon — ground troops and mass displacement
After months of escalating strikes, Israel launched a full ground invasion of Lebanon in September 2024. Hezbollah's leadership was systematically assassinated — including Hassan Nasrallah — through a combination of precision strikes and, famously, supply-chain sabotage (the pager attack). More than 1.2 million Lebanese were displaced. A ceasefire was brokered in November 2024 but remained fragile.
Precedent: supply-chain sabotage as an act of war against a civilian population
2024
Israel · Iran
Direct Israeli strikes on Iranian territory — a first in the conflict's history
In October 2024, Israel launched direct missile strikes on Iranian territory — the first time a foreign military had struck Iran since the Iran-Iraq war. The strikes targeted air defence systems. Iran had previously struck Israel directly in April 2024 following an Israeli strike on an Iranian consulate in Damascus. The tit-for-tat escalation between two sovereign states — conducted without formal declarations of war — established a new normal for interstate military action.
Precedent: direct strikes on sovereign states are now an established tool below the threshold of war
2025
United States · Greenland / Denmark
Trump threatens to "take" Greenland from a NATO ally — by force if necessary
In January 2025, President Trump explicitly stated the US was not ruling out military or economic force to acquire Greenland from Denmark — a fellow NATO member. This was not a diplomatic abstraction. It was a direct territorial claim against an ally backed by an implicit military threat. Denmark's prime minister called the position "unacceptable" and convened emergency meetings with European allies to reaffirm territorial integrity. The episode was widely reported in Western media as an eccentricity. In Beijing, Moscow, and Pyongyang, it was filed as something else entirely: evidence that the rules-based international order's primary guarantor had abandoned it.
Precedent: territorial acquisition by force is back on the table — from the West's own leader

The cascade effect

None of these events is a direct cause of conflicts elsewhere. The causal link is subtler and more durable: they erode the norm. When China's leadership watches the US threaten to seize Greenland and face no meaningful consequence from its allies, it updates its model of what is permissible regarding Taiwan. When Russia watches Israel strike Iran directly and face no international legal accountability, it notes that sovereignty is enforced selectively.

Wars do not happen because leaders are inspired by others' wars. They happen when leaders calculate that the cost of war is acceptable. That calculation is shaped by what they observe. The precedents being set by Western democracies — however justified by their perpetrators — are reducing the perceived cost of aggression globally.

Thesis 4

The Climate Multiplier — the world is running out of easy conditions for peace

Climate change does not start wars. It degrades the conditions under which conflicts can be prevented. It shrinks the resource base that governments need to maintain legitimacy. It displaces populations into already-fragile cities and border regions. It hands militant groups a ready-made recruitment argument — your government failed you, the land is dying, join us. And it does all of this fastest and hardest in the regions that are already closest to the edge.

The IPCC now reflects a consensus: climate shocks amplify the well-documented drivers of conflict — poverty, economic instability, political marginalisation, agricultural dependence. A moderate worsening of climate conditions increases intergroup violence by approximately 2.5% (Burke et al.). With 2–4× that warming projected, conflict risk from climate alone could rise 5–10%. But the compounding effect is what matters most: climate is one of four structural forces now running simultaneously. It is the one that cannot be negotiated with.

Three pathways from climate stress to conflict

01
Resource competition
Drought and desertification shrink arable land and water access. Herding communities push south into farming territory. Violence follows the resource line — not because people are inherently violent, but because there is no longer enough land for both.
02
Displacement and recruitment
Climate displacement floods cities and border regions with impoverished, traumatised people who have lost their land and their state. Militant groups recruit directly from this pool. The Sahel's jihadist expansion maps almost exactly onto its displacement corridors.
03
State fragility
Climate shocks hit weakest states hardest, destroying agricultural tax bases, overwhelming health systems, and generating grievances the government cannot address. The space left behind — ungoverned, resource-stripped — is where armed groups establish themselves.

Case study — The Sahel

The coups had a prehistory the news didn't cover

The wave of military coups that swept Mali, Guinea, Burkina Faso, and Niger between 2020 and 2023 is covered in this page under the power shift — as evidence of declining Western influence. That is accurate. But it is incomplete. Those governments fell partly because they had already failed their populations on the most basic level: food, land, and physical safety. That failure had a climate prehistory stretching back decades.

The Sahel's temperatures are rising 1.5× faster than the global average. The Lake Chad basin — once the sixth-largest lake in the world — has shrunk by over 90% since the 1960s, displacing tens of millions and eliminating the fishing and agricultural economies of four countries. The Sahara is advancing south at an estimated 48 kilometres per year in some areas. Niger loses 100,000 to 120,000 hectares of arable land every year to desertification — land that simply disappears from the agricultural base.

As land degrades, Tuareg and Fulani herders have pushed south into the farming territories of settled communities. These farmer-pastoralist clashes are documented across Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, Nigeria, and Ghana — and they predate the jihadist expansion. What JNIM and ISGS did was weaponise existing grievances that climate had created: offering protection to one community against another, and exploiting the ethnic dimension of land disputes. By the time the coups happened, these governments were already failing to govern the land their people were fighting over.

Why the combination is worse than the sum of the parts

Each of the four structural forces in this analysis would be serious on its own. Together, they form a feedback loop. Climate stress weakens states — and weak states are exactly where the power shift's "no-credible-cost zone" applies. When a government collapses under combined climate and jihadist pressure, the West has neither the leverage to sanction effectively nor the political will to intervene militarily. The vacuum is filled by Russia and China, whose interest is transactional, not developmental. The state gets weaker still.

Meanwhile, democratic precedents — wars launched without UN mandates, international law ignored when inconvenient — remove the normative pressure that might otherwise restrain regional powers from exploiting climate-weakened neighbours. The four forces are not independent. They amplify each other.

Overlooked conflicts

The Forgotten

While Western media coverage focused on Ukraine and Gaza, two of the most destructive conflicts of the 2020s unfolded with almost no sustained international attention. Sudan and Myanmar together account for more than 20 million displaced people — roughly the population of Australia.

Sudan · Since April 2023
The world's largest displacement crisis
Khartoum · Darfur · El Fasher · Port Sudan

On April 15, 2023, fighting broke out between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) — a paramilitary group that emerged from the Janjaweed militias responsible for the Darfur genocide of the 2000s. Within days, the capital Khartoum became a battleground.


The conflict rapidly spread across the country. In Darfur, the RSF conducted mass atrocities against non-Arab communities, with the UN and multiple governments declaring that acts of genocide were taking place in El Geneina and surrounding areas. The city was razed. Tens of thousands were killed in Darfur alone.


By early 2025, Sudan had become the world's largest displacement crisis — surpassing Ukraine. Famine was officially declared in multiple regions in 2024, the first famine declaration anywhere in the world in years. International response has been minimal. No major power has intervened. The UN Security Council remains divided. The war continues.

Myanmar · Since February 2021
A military junta losing a war it cannot stop
Rakhine · Shan · Sagaing · Mandalay

The February 2021 military coup that overthrew Aung San Suu Kyi's elected government triggered a nationwide resistance. What began as street protests evolved into one of the most complex multi-front civil wars in Asia. The People's Defence Forces (PDF), aligned with the National Unity Government (NUG), launched guerrilla operations across the country.


In late 2023, a coordinated offensive by three ethnic armed organisations — the Three Brotherhood Alliance — inflicted the most significant territorial losses the junta has suffered, capturing key border towns and trade routes. By early 2025, the junta controlled less than half of Myanmar's territory — a remarkable reversal for a military that has held power through force for decades.


The human cost has been catastrophic. The military has conducted systematic airstrikes on civilian areas, burned villages, and carried out mass executions. International response has been largely rhetorical. ASEAN's "Five-Point Consensus" — the regional body's response — has produced no measurable change. China, which borders Myanmar, has maintained ties with both the junta and some ethnic armed groups, prioritising stability over accountability.

What this means

The Cost — what a world with more war looks like

The numbers in this analysis — 61 conflicts, 59 countries affected by terrorism, 123 million displaced — are not abstractions. They are the aggregate of individual lives destroyed, economies hollowed out, and political systems pushed to collapse. This is what the trend toward more conflict, left unchecked, produces. All figures are current as of 2024–2025.

Casualties
160k
deaths in organised violence · 2024
That is the official UCDP count for deaths in state-based armed conflict — a figure that excludes the tens of thousands more killed in non-state violence, gang conflict, and one-sided attacks on civilians. Ukraine alone accounted for ~76,000 battle deaths. The true toll of all organised violence in 2024 is significantly higher. Civilian deaths rose 31% year-on-year.
Displacement
123M
forcibly displaced worldwide at end of 2024
1 in every 67 people on Earth is currently displaced by conflict, persecution, or violence. That number has risen every single year for over a decade. 73.5 million are internally displaced — unable to cross a border, trapped inside their own failing state. Sudan alone accounts for 11.6 million — the largest internal displacement crisis ever recorded.
Hunger & malnutrition
295M
facing acute hunger across 53 countries · 2024
Acute food insecurity rose for the sixth consecutive year in 2024. Conflict is the primary driver, affecting 140 million people in 20 countries. 1.9 million face catastrophic hunger (IPC Phase 5 — one step from famine) — the highest number ever recorded, having doubled in a single year. Conflict disrupts harvests, destroys supply chains, and blocks aid. In Sudan, 80% of aid kitchens have closed.
Economic cost
$19T
total economic impact of violence · 2024
13.5% of global GDP — roughly the entire economic output of the United States — is consumed annually by the direct and indirect costs of violence. This includes $9 trillion in military spending (up $540 billion in 2024 alone), $462 billion in GDP losses directly caused by active conflict (up 44% in a single year), and $343 billion in the cost of refugees and displaced people. Money spent on war cannot be spent on health, education, or climate adaptation.
Terrorism & militant expansion
66
countries experiencing terrorism in 2024 · up from 58
The Global Terrorism Index 2025 found that 45 countries deteriorated and only 34 improved — the first time more countries worsened than improved in seven years. Islamic State now operates in 22 countries. The Sahel accounts for over half of all terrorism-related deaths globally. 98% of all terrorist deaths occur in active conflict zones. Conflict zones are militant group incubators: ungoverned space, displaced recruits, and weapons in circulation.
Refugees & migration pressure
8.4M
asylum-seekers awaiting decisions · record high · 2024
8.4 million people were waiting on asylum decisions at the end of 2024 — a 22% increase and the highest number ever recorded. Refugee flows from conflict zones are straining host governments across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. The political reaction — rising anti-immigration sentiment, border militarisation, far-right electoral gains — is reshaping democracies far from the original conflicts. War exports its consequences to countries that never chose to be involved.

Why these consequences make the next war more likely

The consequences of conflict are not merely the cost of the wars that are happening. They are the conditions that produce the next ones. Displaced populations in fragile host countries create political pressure that destabilises governments. Hunger strips agricultural communities of the livelihoods that kept them from turning to armed groups. Economic damage in conflict-adjacent countries — trade disruption, energy price spikes, capital flight — weakens institutions and creates grievances. Terrorism from one ungoverned zone spreads to neighbouring states.

The $19 trillion annual cost of violence also directly competes with the spending needed to address the other structural drivers in this analysis. Every dollar spent on military procurement is a dollar not spent on climate adaptation in a vulnerable Sahel community. Every billion consumed by reconstruction after a preventable war is a billion not spent building the state capacity that makes the next coup less likely.

→
Displacement destabilises host countries, fuels anti-immigration politics, and weakens the democratic coalitions most likely to resist future conflicts
→
Hunger removes the economic stake that gives communities reason to avoid conflict — people with nothing left to lose are easier to recruit
→
Terrorism from conflict zones spreads to neighbouring states, drawing them into conflicts they did not choose and creating new theatres of instability
→
Economic damage reduces state capacity in conflict-adjacent countries, bringing more governments closer to the fragility threshold where coups and civil war become probable

"In 2024, violence cost the world $19 trillion — enough to eliminate global extreme poverty seventeen times over. The question is not whether the world can afford to prevent conflict. It is whether it has calculated the cost of not doing so."

Sources and methodology

State-based armed conflict definition: organised use of armed force by a state party, causing at least 25 battle-related deaths in a calendar year. 2025–2035 figures are author projections based on 2020–2024 trend rate and should be treated as indicative, not predictive. ACLED conflict event data covers 1 Dec 2024–Nov 2025.

Economic data: IMF World Economic Outlook Database (October 2024 edition). GDP figures use purchasing power parity (PPP) to allow cross-country comparison. G7 defined as Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, United Kingdom, United States.

Humanitarian & consequences data: UNHCR Global Trends 2024 (displacement, asylum figures); Global Report on Food Crises 2025 — FAO/WFP/UNICEF (hunger and malnutrition); Global Terrorism Index 2025 — Institute for Economics & Peace (terrorism country counts, militant group expansion); Institute for Economics & Peace Global Peace Index 2024 (economic cost of violence). Casualty figures for active conflicts are estimates; conflict zones make independent verification extremely difficult. All figures are the most recent credible estimates available as of March 2026.
