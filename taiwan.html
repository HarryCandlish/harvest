The Pacific — 2026

A relationship built over 50 years — now at the centre of the most dangerous scenario in the region

In 2008, New Zealand became the first developed nation to sign a Free Trade Agreement with China. It was considered a diplomatic triumph. Eighteen years later, that relationship is the single biggest variable in how a Taiwan conflict would land on New Zealand — economically, politically, and strategically. The closure mechanism · Days, not months

This channel does not taper — it closes. We know this because it happened to Japan four months ago.

In November 2025, Japanese PM Takaichi stated that a Chinese attack on Taiwan could constitute an "existential crisis for Japan." China issued a travel advisory within days. Major Chinese travel agencies halted Japan bookings. By December 2025, Chinese arrivals to Japan had fallen 45% year-on-year — from one of Japan's largest visitor markets to a near-shutdown in weeks. Japan's estimated loss: ¥57 billion ($600M NZD) in a single month, rising to ¥2 trillion if sustained. The mechanism is identical for New Zealand: a government advisory suspends Air New Zealand's Shanghai and Beijing routes, cancels group tours, and removes NZ from approved destination lists. New Zealand would lose $1.2–1.5B in visitor revenue within weeks, not quarters, falling entirely on operators who cannot pivot to other markets. South Korea lost 48% of its tourism to China within two months during the 2017 THAAD dispute — Japan 2025 shows the same playbook, updated.

The NZD · The universal transmission mechanism

The NZD falls in every global risk event. A sustained depreciation converts macroeconomic shock into household inflation.

The New Zealand dollar is a high-beta currency — it falls faster than most in global risk-off events because NZ is a small, trade-dependent economy with significant offshore borrowing. That makes all NZ imports more expensive — petrol, electronics, food — adding 2–3 percentage points to consumer price inflation at exactly the moment the economy is weakest.

Three drivers · Simultaneous inflation

In a normal inflation episode one or two drivers operate. A Taiwan conflict triggers all three at once — and they amplify each other.

New Zealand has experienced each of these inflation drivers in isolation: the NZD fell sharply in 2008 and 2020; oil spiked in 2008 and 2022; supply chains tightened in 2020–2022. In each case, the economy absorbed one or two pressures at a time. A Taiwan conflict combines all three simultaneously — NZD depreciation makes all imports more expensive, oil prices spike as regional conflict disrupts shipping and triggers precautionary buying, and supply chain tightening pushes costs up from the supply side. The result is inflationary pressure that is broader, faster, and harder to resolve than any single-driver episode.

The RBNZ dilemma · Raising rates into a recession

Stagflation — high inflation plus a contracting economy — is the scenario the RBNZ has no good answer to.

The RBNZ's primary mandate is price stability. If inflation runs at 8–10% — which the combined drivers could produce — the Bank is obligated to keep the OCR elevated, or raise it further. But the economy is simultaneously contracting: export revenues are down, businesses are cutting investment, and unemployment is rising. Cutting rates to support growth would add fuel to inflation. Holding rates to fight inflation deepens the recession. Every 100bps the OCR is held higher than the economy can sustain costs NZ households approximately $400 million per year in additional mortgage payments.

Will China actually try to reunify Taiwan? And what does the evidence say?

The short answer: Xi Jinping has said so, publicly and repeatedly, in the language of historical inevitability. The PLA has built the tools. What remains uncertain is the timing — and whether coercion short of war might work first.

The case against — why it may never happen

01

The Taiwan Strait is a nightmare to cross

The strait is 180 km wide at its narrowest — deeper and rougher than the English Channel, battered by typhoons June through October, and swept by strong currents that make precision amphibious landings exceptionally difficult. Taiwan's viable landing beaches are few, well-mapped, and heavily fortified. The last successful large-scale opposed amphibious assault in history was Inchon, 1950 — regarded as a miracle of timing and planning. No modern military has attempted anything close to the scale a Taiwan invasion would require.

02

China's economy is already under severe strain

China entered 2026 in deflation: factory-gate prices falling 3.6% year-on-year, a property sector in freefall (development investment down 16%), private investment contracting 5.3%, and youth unemployment at 18.9% — a record. A war would trigger Western sanctions that could collapse exports, freeze dollar reserves, and detonate an already fragile financial system at the worst possible moment. Xi is a nationalist, but he is also a technocrat who has spent 14 years consolidating economic control. He knows what a losing war would cost the Party's legitimacy.

03

The American problem has no clean solution

The US 7th Fleet is the largest forward-deployed naval force in the world. US treaty alliances with Japan and South Korea put advanced air bases and missile batteries within minutes of the strait. AUKUS adds Australian submarine capacity to the Pacific equation. China would be fighting the most capable military in history in the confined maritime space where US carrier-based air power and submarine warfare are most effective. PLA war games model American intervention as the decisive variable — and no scenario produces a clean Chinese victory with full US involvement.

04

Xi has purged his own military — twice — for corruption

Since mid-2023, Xi has removed over 15 senior military and defence officials, including two consecutive Defence Ministers and the entire leadership of the PLA Rocket Force. The reason: systemic corruption. US officials reported that some missiles were found filled with water instead of fuel, and silo lids in western China were fitted with faulty mechanisms that would prevent launch in a crisis. Xi cannot be certain his military would perform as advertised. The purges signal he isn't certain either.

05

The PLA has not fought a war since 1979

China's last armed conflict was the Sino-Vietnamese War — a brief, costly campaign against a far smaller neighbour that China officially "won" but which exposed serious command and logistics failures. That was 47 years ago. No serving PLA general has commanded troops in combat. No joint amphibious operation has been rehearsed under live fire. Simulations and exercises are not the same as war. The US military — with continuous combat experience across Iraq, Afghanistan, and persistent counterterrorism operations — has institutionalised lessons the PLA can only model theoretically.

06

Russia couldn't take a weaker country across a shared land border

Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine — an operation planners expected to take 72 hours — has ground on for years against a country with a fraction of NATO's military support and no water between them. Logistics failures, poor unit cohesion, and the stubborn effectiveness of a motivated defender shattered assumptions about modern conventional war. Taiwan is not Ukraine: it is an island, with a professional military, significant air defences, modern anti-ship missiles, and $11.1 billion in new US arms approved in 2025. A maritime invasion attempt would face every challenge Russia faced on land — and then the strait.

The honest assessment: Xi Jinping almost certainly wants reunification. He is less certain he can win it militarily — and he may calculate that economic coercion, political pressure, and the credible threat of force can achieve what an invasion cannot. The risk for New Zealand is that this calculus can change. A miscalculation, a provocation, a domestic political crisis in Beijing — any of these can turn a deterrence posture into a war.

For the purposes of this model — the miscalculation happens. Scroll to the moment the strait is sealed.

Strategic ambiguity as deterrent
The US has never confirmed whether it would intervene militarily — because the threat of intervention is worth more than certainty. The first island chain
Taiwan sits at the centre of the arc of islands — from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines — that contains Chinese naval power in the Western Pacific. Japan and South Korea going nuclear

Before you decide on sanctions — understand what they would actually do

The Russia parallel has limits. When the West sanctioned Russia, China continued trading — buying Russian oil at a discount and filling the gap left by Western firms. For New Zealand, the sanctions calculus is particularly asymmetric. NZ represents roughly 0.2% of global GDP. Its participation in a sanctions coalition would contribute almost nothing to the economic pressure on China. But the sanctions themselves — by disrupting global supply chains, weakening NZ's trading partners, and accelerating inflation through a falling NZD and higher import costs — would damage the New Zealand economy through channels entirely separate from China's retaliation. Sanctions are a weapon designed for countries that are peripheral to your supply chain. Russia was peripheral. China is not. The decision below is not simply about values versus trade — it is about whether NZ's symbolic participation in a sanctions regime meaningfully changes its outcome, or whether it primarily determines how much of the economic damage NZ absorbs directly versus indirectly.

Beyond the model

A Chinese military takeover of Taiwan — what Beijing calls reunification, what the West calls an occupation — would be the largest geopolitical disruption since 1945. New Zealand is not prepared for it.

The numbers in this model are the floor, not the ceiling

The economic figures shown throughout this scenario capture only direct trade exposure — lost export revenue if China retaliates or its economy contracts. They do not include the full macroeconomic cascade. A conflict of this scale would trigger inflation through disrupted supply chains and a falling NZD. Business investment would freeze. KiwiSaver and superannuation funds would take significant hits as global markets reprice risk. Chinese tourism — worth roughly \$1.5B annually — would end immediately. The housing market, already fragile, would face sustained pressure as credit tightened and confidence collapsed.

When all channels are included — trade, tourism, education, supply chain disruption, inflation, exchange rate, investment, and financial market exposure — the full economic impact of the worst-case scenario could be two to three times the trade figures alone. For New Zealanders, that is not an abstraction. It is mortgages, grocery bills, medicine shortages, and jobs.

The time to think about this is not when the crisis arrives

New Zealand has national security frameworks — a National Security Strategy, a Defence Capability Plan, active Five Eyes commitments. What it does not have is anything comparable to Australia's Defence Strategic Review (2023) or National Defence Strategy (2024), both of which explicitly name the Indo-Pacific threat environment, model capability requirements against specific contingencies, and lay out a published framework for the decisions a government would face. New Zealand's equivalent documents are carefully worded. The word "Taiwan" does not appear as a planning scenario in any of them.

The decisions explored in this model — public statement, UNGA vote, coalition posture, naval response, defence spending — would all need to be made under acute time pressure, without the benefit of prior public deliberation, in a media environment moving faster than any Cabinet process. Countries that navigate crises well are countries that have thought about them in advance. Not predicted them — thought about them. Explored the trade-offs. Understood the costs of each path before the phone rings at 3am. This model is an attempt to make those costs visible before the moment they become unavoidable.
