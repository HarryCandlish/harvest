2026 Election Analysis · AI-guided insight

Finding signal
in the noise

With New Zealand's 2026 general election approaching, I scraped press releases from all six major parties and ran keyword analysis across eight policy topics to see where each party is choosing to focus. The results reveal starkly different narratives.

One of AI's most powerful capabilities is finding patterns inside large volumes of unstructured text — the kind of data that's too slow to read manually and too messy for a spreadsheet. Party press releases, customer reviews, support tickets, meeting notes: AI can scan thousands of documents in seconds, extract the topics that matter, and surface insight that would otherwise stay buried.

Here the approach was deliberately transparent: scrape the raw text, define keyword categories before analysis to avoid bias, normalise by word count so volume differences don't skew the comparison, then visualise. Raw text, keyword counts, word-frequency normalisation. No calls on what matters — the data decides what's being talked about.

03 — Key findings

What the data tells us

Opposition parties naturally attack government record — so Labour's heavy focus on economy, health and housing reflects the standard playbook. But the scale of the gaps, National's near-silence on environment, and NZ First's narrow but consistent focus on immigration and sovereignty all stand out.

Economy · Everyone's talking
Dominant topic

Economy and cost of living are the highest-frequency topics across all six parties. Labour (11.43) leads, but Green (3.50) and NZ First (3.94) both outpace National (6.05) on economic language — the opposition is louder on the economy than the government managing it.

Environment · Opposition vs Coalition
10× gap

Labour and the Greens average 3.97 environment mentions per 1,000 words. National, NZ First and ACT average 0.41. The three governing parties are near-silent on climate. The Greens (3.39) and Labour (4.56) carry almost all the environmental messaging between them.

Housing · Three parties
Te Pāti Māori #1

Te Pāti Māori leads every party on housing at 7.38 per 1,000 words, framing it as a Māori and Tiriti issue. Labour (6.83) and Green (3.50) follow. National sits at 2.03 — the party that campaigned on fixing housing is the quietest governing voice on the topic. TPM figure based on 8 releases — indicative.

The full picture
Six parties, one story

Across 287 releases, the pattern is consistent: opposition parties talk more, about more topics, more loudly. The governing coalition — National, NZ First, ACT — keeps its messaging tight and topic-narrow. Whether that's discipline or defensiveness is a question the election will answer.

Immigration · NZ First
NZ First owns it

NZ First leads all parties on immigration at 1.65 per 1,000 words — ahead of National (1.38) and ACT (0.16). Labour, Green and Te Pāti Māori barely register on the topic. NZ First is the only party making immigration a consistent messaging priority in 2026.

Healthcare · Incumbent disadvantage
2.3× Labour lead

Labour mentions health 2.3 times more than National — hospital waitlists, Pharmac cuts, mental health funding. National has little incentive to amplify a topic where they're on the back foot. Notably, Te Pāti Māori (0.74) is quieter than expected given Māori health disparities are a documented priority.

Crime · Contested ground
Most evenly spread

Crime is the most evenly distributed topic across all parties — National (1.72), NZ First (1.47), Te Pāti Māori (4.06), Labour (2.28), ACT (0.82). Everyone has a crime narrative, but they're rarely talking about the same thing: public safety, sentencing, gang policy and systemic incarceration sit in very different conversations.

Education · Unexpected leaders
ACT 3.68 · TPM 3.32

ACT tops all parties on education at 3.68 per 1,000 words, followed closely by Te Pāti Māori at 3.32 — two parties with entirely opposing visions for schooling. National (2.71) sits in the middle while Labour barely registers. Charter schools and curriculum reform versus kaupapa Māori — the same topic, opposite directions.

What this means

Turning analysis into action

For parties & strategists

Messaging gaps are measurable vulnerabilities. National's near-silence on housing and environment — two of voters' top concerns — creates a quantifiable space for opposition parties to fill. Communications teams can use this kind of analysis to identify where they are ceding narrative ground before an election campaign begins.

For journalists & researchers

Normalising by word count gives a fairer basis for comparing party activity than raw volume — a small party publishing fewer releases isn't quieter on an issue, just smaller. This methodology can be applied to any corpus of political text: speeches, Hansard debates, social media posts, or post-election policy documents.

For voters

What a party talks about most is a reasonable proxy for what it will prioritise in government. This data cuts through campaign messaging to show revealed priorities — the topics each party chose to put words behind, unprompted, over several months. That's a more reliable signal than a manifesto written for an election.
