Wellington's story

The hardest fall of any region

Wellington fell from $950,000 to $730,000 — a 23% decline, the steepest of any NZ region. Its housing market tracks public sector employment more closely than anywhere else in the country, and 2023–2024 delivered a double blow: the fastest rate cycle in NZ history, and mass redundancies across the public service.

What the correction didn't fix

Prices fell. Affordability didn't recover.

Bubble size now shows how far each region is still above its 2015 price. Every region is 60–80% higher than where it started. The correction reversed the Covid surge. It didn't reverse the decade.

Northland · 4.1% deprivation

The highest deprivation rate in the country

Northland's housing crisis predates the boom — but the boom made it worse. Whangārei median prices surged from ~$300k to ~$700k, and rents followed. For a region with persistent unemployment and wages well below the national average, the affordability gap became unbridgeable. Many households rely on aging, cold, and damp stock that was never upgraded. The correction brought prices down slightly. It brought rents down by almost nothing.

Source: Stats NZ 2023 Census. Deprivation includes severe crowding, damaged dwellings, and lack of basic amenities.

Gisborne · 3.8% deprivation

Isolated, agricultural, and left behind by every cycle

Gisborne is one of NZ's most geographically isolated cities, with an economy built on seasonal agriculture and a population that is around 50% Māori. House prices at the peak reached ~$500k — extraordinary relative to local incomes. Multigenerational crowding became the norm as rental supply dried up. Unlike Auckland or Wellington, there was no correction driven by investor retreat: Gisborne never attracted the speculative demand that drives those markets back down.

Source: Stats NZ 2023 Census.

West Coast · 3.6% deprivation

Affordable prices. Unaffordable conditions.

The West Coast is an outlier: house prices here remain among the lowest in the country, yet deprivation is among the highest. The crisis isn't about price — it's about housing quality, availability, and a shrinking local economy. Young people leave for better opportunities. What remains is an aging population, old mining and forestry housing stock that is cold, damp, and expensive to heat, and a rental market so thin that moving town for work means finding nowhere to live.

Source: Stats NZ 2023 Census.

Looking ahead · 2026

There are reasons, cautiously, to be hopeful

The decade from 2012 to 2022 was defined by a single belief: property only goes up. That belief shaped how New Zealanders saved, invested, and thought about their futures. The data suggests it may finally be softening — and that a few structural forces are, for the first time in years, pointing in the right direction.

Prices have corrected

The 15–23% falls from peak are real. For buyers who sat out the boom, entry prices are meaningfully lower than they were in 2022. Not back to 2015 — but the gap between incomes and prices has narrowed for the first time this decade.

Rates are falling

The OCR has come down sharply from its 5.50% peak. Mortgage rates are following. For homeowners who fixed at the top of the cycle, refinancing onto lower rates is easing the pressure — and improving serviceability for prospective buyers.

Supply is finally moving

Consents remain elevated by historical standards. Infill development, medium-density reforms, and a more permissive planning environment in Auckland and Wellington are beginning to add real stock to markets that spent a decade starved of it.

A cultural shift, quietly underway

Investors are increasingly looking past the second property. Shares, index funds, and private equity are attracting capital that a decade ago would have gone into a rental. The tax and regulatory environment for landlords has tightened — and for many, the numbers no longer stack up the way they once did.

For Northland, Gisborne & the West Coast

The four signals above — falling prices, lower rates, new supply, shifting investment culture — are mostly stories about Auckland and Wellington. They don't automatically reach the regions where deprivation is highest. The honest picture there is more complicated. But there are some genuine grounds for guarded hope.

Iwi-led housing

In Northland and Gisborne, Māori land trusts and iwi development arms are increasingly building housing on Māori land — outside the mainstream market. These projects don't show up in consent data, but they're adding real homes for whānau who can't access the private market.

Connectivity improving

Roading improvements into Northland and the slow expansion of digital infrastructure are beginning to make remote work viable in regions that previously had no alternative to local employment. A small but real shift — and one that brings spending power into communities that have been losing it for decades.

Social housing focus

Lower land costs in Gisborne and the West Coast mean that social housing providers — Kāinga Ora and community housing trusts — can build more for the same budget than in main centres. If funding follows need rather than political salience, these are the regions that should benefit most.

⚠
The caveat

All of this is contingent. The conflict in Iran has already pushed oil prices higher and introduced inflationary pressure that central banks — including the RBNZ — will need to weigh against cutting further. If inflation re-accelerates, the rate relief that underpins this cautious optimism could stall. The direction is right. The pace is uncertain.

What this means

Five things the data is telling housing policymakers

01
The correction was real — but it only undid two years of gains.Every region fell 15–23% from its 2022 peak. Prices remain 60–80% above 2015 levels across the country. The correction reversed the Covid surge. It didn't reverse the decade. For first-home buyers, the window narrowed slightly — it didn't open.
02
Wellington's vulnerability reflects economic concentration — not just rate exposure.Wellington's 23% fall is the steepest of any region. Its housing market tracks public sector employment more closely than any other NZ city. Fiscal tightening, redundancies, and higher rates compounded into the country's largest correction. Policy risk and market risk aligned at the same moment.
03
Deprivation and price are two different crises — and they don't overlap where you'd expect.Northland (4.1%), Gisborne (3.8%), and West Coast (3.6%) have the highest deprivation rates. None are among the most expensive markets. These communities were underserved before the boom, through it, and after it. Policies focused on cooling price growth don't reach them.
04
The OCR is a blunt instrument — it hits every region equally but labour markets aren't equal.Raising rates to cool Auckland and Wellington also raised mortgage costs for households in Northland and Gisborne — regions with lower median incomes and thinner employment buffers. The tool was right for the cities where the problem was hottest. The cost was shared by regions that barely participated in the boom.
05
The rate cycle moderated demand. It didn't add a single house.As the OCR falls back toward neutral, demand will return. If supply hasn't materially increased in the regions where people want to live, prices will recover — from a higher floor. The window to build more housing is exactly the period when prices are softening. NZ has not consistently used it.
