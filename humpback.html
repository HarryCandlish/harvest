01

1827 – 1964

Hunted to the edge of extinction

New Zealand's first shore whaling station opened at Te Awaiti in Tory Channel in 1827 — one of the country's earliest European settlements. Whangamumu in the Bay of Islands followed in 1898. For 137 years, humpbacks were hunted on their winter migration through Cook Strait and along the North Island coast.

The broader Pacific fleet — American, British, Norwegian, and Soviet — compounded the damage across the entire Oceania stock. Click the red station markers to explore each site's history.

02

By the 1960s

A sea emptied of whales

By the time New Zealand's last shore station at Tory Channel closed in 1964 — not by law, but because the whales had run out — the Oceania humpback population had collapsed to an estimated 200–400 animals. Down from tens of thousands before commercial whaling began.

Cook Strait, once a reliable migration corridor where shore boats could reliably find humpbacks each winter, fell nearly silent.

03

1986

The turning point

In 1986, the International Whaling Commission enacted a global moratorium on commercial whaling. For the first time in 160 years, humpbacks in the South Pacific could complete their migration without being hunted.

Recovery would not be fast — a population depleted over generations cannot rebound overnight. But the moratorium was the turning point. The hunting stopped. The counting began.

04

1970 – 1999

Scientists start listening again

Survey teams began recording humpback sightings in New Zealand waters from the early 1970s — tentative at first, a handful of confirmed encounters each year. These dots represent real survey records compiled from DOC and NZ university research programmes.

Sparse compared to what was once here. But growing.

05

Annual migration

6,000 km each way

Each winter, humpbacks leave their Antarctic feeding grounds and travel north — through New Zealand's coastal waters, past the Kermadec Islands, to the warm breeding lagoons of Tonga and the Cook Islands. The return journey south, in spring, follows the same corridors.

It is one of the longest migrations of any mammal on Earth. NZ sits at the heart of it — the same geography that made these waters so productive for whalers is what makes them so important for recovery.

06

2000 – Present

They're back

Today, humpback sightings in New Zealand waters are no longer rare. Records from Happywhale, iNaturalist, and citizen science surveys cluster along the Kaikōura coast, in Cook Strait, and off the Bay of Islands — the same corridors where they were once hunted.

The Oceania population has grown from ~200 animals in 1986 to an estimated 4,000–5,000 today, and is still climbing. In 2016, the population was reclassified from endangered to recovering.

Conservation

One of conservation's genuine success stories

The Oceania humpback was reclassified from "endangered" to "recovering" in 2016 — a milestone that took 30 years of protection and patient population monitoring to achieve. No single intervention produced the recovery. The moratorium held. The whales returned.

The recovery is not complete. Some sub-stocks within Oceania remain at lower levels. Threats from ship strike, fishing gear entanglement, and climate change affecting Antarctic krill abundance remain real. But the trajectory is clear.

A species that was commercially hunted to near-extinction has returned to New Zealand waters within a single human lifetime. That matters — not just for the whales, but as evidence that collapse is not always irreversible, and that protection, given time, works.
