The Ledger · Liveability analysis · 2025

A case for Wellington.
Why number 20 undersells it.

Wellington ranked 20th in the 2025 EIU Global Liveability Index — its lowest placing in a decade. But the index has a structural bias that systematically disadvantages smaller cities on two of its five categories. When you account for population size, Wellington's true liveability may sit several places higher.

Background · The Economist Intelligence Unit

What the Global Liveability Index actually measures

Since 2002, the Economist Intelligence Unit has published an annual ranking of the world's most liveable cities. The index assesses 173 cities across five categories, each weighted by importance. The resulting score — out of 100 — determines global rank. It's used by multinational companies to calculate hardship allowances for employees relocated abroad, which explains its design: it measures liveability from the perspective of an expatriate professional, not a local resident.

That design choice matters. The index is not asking "is this a good place to grow up, age, or raise a family on a local salary?" It's asking "is this a comfortable, safe, functional city for someone arriving from elsewhere?" Both questions are worth answering — but they don't always point to the same place.

Two of those five categories — Culture & Environment (25%) and Infrastructure (20%) — account for 45% of the total score. Both are sensitive to city size in ways that disadvantage smaller populations. A city of 200,000 will always offer fewer international transport routes, fewer major sporting events, and a smaller restaurant and cultural scene than a city of 2 million — regardless of how well it performs on those measures per capita.

Historical context · EIU data via Wikipedia

Wellington's ranking over time

Wellington has featured in or near the top 20 for much of the past decade, broadly tracking Auckland but consistently ranking several places lower. The pandemic years produced a notable anomaly: cities that handled COVID well — including both New Zealand cities — briefly climbed the rankings in 2020 and 2021, before falling back as European cities recovered. Wellington's slide to 20th in 2025 reflects both a normalisation of that pandemic advantage and the continued rise of European competitors.

The structural argument · Population & index bias

The index has a sweet spot — and Wellington sits outside it

Look at the 2025 top 20 and one pattern stands out: the majority of ranked cities have populations between 500,000 and 3 million. That range is the index's sweet spot — large enough to support a full cultural and transport offering, small enough to maintain low crime and high liveability. Wellington, at around 215,000 people (city proper), sits well below that band. So do Geneva and Luxembourg City — both of which rank higher than Wellington despite similar or smaller populations.

The chart reveals the population spread clearly. Most top-20 cities cluster between 0.5M and 3M. Tokyo is the extreme outlier at the high end — it makes the list despite 14 million residents, driven by exceptional infrastructure and a uniquely safe urban environment. At the low end, Geneva (5th) and Luxembourg City (11th) both beat Wellington despite smaller populations — but both benefit from extraordinary wealth, international institutions, and EU connectivity that inflates their scores on categories where small size would otherwise hurt them.

Where small cities are penalised

Culture & Environment · Infrastructure
45% of total score

Fewer international flights and direct long-haul routes. Less frequent public transport. A smaller calendar of major sporting events, international concerts, and cultural festivals. A more limited restaurant scene by sheer variety. These are structural realities of a city of 215,000 — not failures of governance or ambition.

Wellington has one international airport with limited direct routes, a bus network that has improved significantly but lacks the frequency of European comparators, and a cultural calendar that punches above its weight — but still cannot match cities three to five times its size.

Where small cities are rewarded

Stability · Healthcare · Education
55% of total score

Violent crime is exceptionally low. Political stability is among the highest in the world — Wellington is the capital of a country that has not experienced a coup, civil conflict, or major political violence in its modern history. Healthcare is publicly funded and accessible.

On the categories that constitute the majority of the index score, Wellington competes directly with the top-ranked European cities. The problem is that the 45% of the score where population matters most pulls the overall number down in ways that have nothing to do with how pleasant the city actually is to live in.

The question the index doesn't answer is: what would Wellington's score look like if you normalised Culture & Environment and Infrastructure for population size? On a per-capita basis, a city that hosts a world-class national museum, a professional rugby team, a symphony orchestra, and an international film industry at 215,000 people is doing something remarkable.

The verdict · Two honest readings

Why it might be higher — and why 20th might be about right

The population bias argument is real, but it doesn't tell the whole story. There are genuine reasons Wellington ranks where it does — and genuine reasons to think the number undersells the place.

The case for higher

The index underweights what Wellington does well at its size

Safety and stability that rival any city in the top 5 — Wellington has world-class scores on crime, political stability, and civil unrest

Cultural output disproportionate to population — Te Papa, Royal NZ Ballet, NZSO, Weta Workshop, and the most restaurants per capita of any NZ city

A compact, walkable city centre where the practical experience of daily life — commute times, access to nature, street safety — is excellent

Healthcare quality comparable to European counterparts, fully publicly funded and accessible without insurance

If Culture & Environment and Infrastructure scores were normalised per capita, Wellington's rank would likely sit several places higher

The case for about right

Housing costs and transport constraints are real penalties

Housing affordability in Wellington has deteriorated sharply — median house prices relative to median income are among the worst in the OECD, rivalling Sydney and Melbourne

Cost of living has risen significantly — groceries, dining, and services are expensive relative to wages, which the index doesn't fully capture

International transport is genuinely limited — no direct routes to Europe or North America, restricted long-haul options for a capital city

Public transport, while improved, still falls well short of European comparators of similar size — reliability and frequency remain issues

The index measures what an expatriate professional experiences. For that person, Wellington's cost and connectivity gaps are real and felt daily

The honest reading is probably this: Wellington at 20th is the correct answer to the question the index is asking — how does it compare as a destination for an international professional? But it is the wrong answer to a different, arguably more important question: for the people who actually live there, how does the daily experience of Wellington compare to the cities ranked above it? On that question, the population bias matters, and the number is almost certainly too low.
