Iran War · 28 Feb 2026 — ongoing

A petrol price driven by war — and a country already under pressure

Every cent added to the litre price in New Zealand is a measure of two things at once. For families already stretched by years of rising costs, it is another hit to the weekly budget — petrol, groceries, freight, everything connected to oil moves with it. And it is something else: a real-time signal from a war 14,000 kilometres away, where the strikes are still happening, the shipping lanes are still closed, and the people caught in the middle of it have nowhere to go.

The Middle East — February 2026

A region on the edge of war

On 28 February 2026, a joint US-Israeli strike force launched one of the largest coordinated air campaigns since the Second World War — targeting military infrastructure and the Iranian leadership in a single night. Scroll through to see how the conflict unfolded, and what it means at the petrol pump in New Zealand.

28 February — Day 1

900 strikes in 12 hours

The opening salvo hit simultaneously across Iran. Supreme Leader Khamenei was killed in the first hours. Nuclear facilities at Natanz, Fordow, Arak and Isfahan were struck by B-2 stealth bombers. Naval bases at Bandar Abbas — the gateway to the Strait of Hormuz — were among the first targets.

Days 2–3 — 1–2 March

Strikes spread to 26 of 31 provinces

Within 48 hours, ACLED had documented strikes across 26 of Iran's 31 provinces. Air bases, missile storage facilities, IRGC command posts, and oil infrastructure were targeted nationwide. The IRGC ordered civilian evacuation of Mariwan, a Kurdish city near the Iraqi border, signalling preparations for possible ground operations.

~800confirmed dead by March 3 — Iranian Red Crescent

Iranian retaliation — 1–4 March

Iran strikes across 10 countries

Iran's response was immediate and regional. Missiles and drones targeted US bases in the Gulf, civilian infrastructure in the UAE (the most heavily struck country), and Israeli cities. Hezbollah launched long-range missiles at Tel Aviv from southern Lebanon. More than 90 Iranian missile and drone attempts targeted Israel — approximately 20 reached civilian areas.

1 March — Strait of Hormuz

The world's most important shipping lane is closed

On 1 March, Iran attacked the first commercial vessel attempting to pass through the Strait of Hormuz — a 21-mile wide channel between Iran and Oman through which 20% of global oil supply flows every day. Within hours, insurers had suspended coverage for vessels transiting the strait. The global oil price responded immediately.

11 March — Commercial shipping

Named vessels struck — 150 ships anchored outside the strait

Iran moved from attacking military targets to commercial shipping. Seven named vessels were struck around the Strait of Hormuz and Persian Gulf. The Mayuree Naree caught fire — 20 crew were rescued by the Omani Navy, three remain missing. Tanker traffic through the strait collapsed by 70%, from over 100 ships a day to a trickle.

The oil shock

Dubai crude surges — New Zealand pays

Dubai crude went from $71/bbl before the war to $115/bbl by March 13 — a 62% rise in less than two weeks. Brent crude followed. The NZD also weakened against the USD, amplifying the shock at the pump. Scroll down to see how this fed through to NZ petrol prices.

13 March — Kharg Island

Iran's economic lifeline — and America's next target

Closing the Strait of Hormuz was a tactical win for Iran — but Trump responded on March 13 by bombing 90+ military targets on Kharg Island. Naval mine storage, missile bunkers, and military infrastructure were destroyed. The oil export terminal was deliberately left intact. Trump's interest in Kharg is not new — in 1988, at the height of the Iran-Iraq tanker war, he told a reporter he would "do a number" on the island if he were in charge. Nearly four decades later, he was — and he did.

The oil export terminal remains standing — deliberately. A direct strike on it would be an economic decapitation of Iran, cutting off 90% of its oil export revenue overnight. Crude prices would reach levels not seen since 2008. Trump knows this. Kharg is leverage — not yet a target, but never fully off the table.

14 March — The war spreads

Iran opens a second front — the Red Sea

With Kharg bombed but still standing, Iran escalated rather than retreated. On 14 March, three tankers bound for Saudi Arabia were struck in the Red Sea — targeting the kingdom through its western shipping corridor. The same day, Iran attempted to hit Fujairah on the UAE east coast, a critical oil storage and bunkering hub that serves tankers bypassing the Gulf. The message was unambiguous: if the Persian Gulf was closed, so would every route around it.

Iran does not need to do this alone. The Houthi movement in Yemen — Iran's most active regional proxy — proved during the Israel-Hamas war that they could shut down Red Sea traffic almost single-handedly. Between November 2023 and early 2024, Houthi drone and missile attacks reduced Red Sea shipping by over 50%, forcing carriers to reroute around southern Africa. That capability has not gone away. With Iranian direction and resupply, the Houthis represent a second front that requires no Iranian ships, no Iranian casualties, and no direct attribution.

Saudi Arabia had not been drawn into the conflict directly — yet. But sustained attacks on Saudi oil infrastructure or Red Sea shipping could bring Riyadh into the war on the US-Israeli side, fundamentally changing the character of the conflict.

16 March — No way out

The death of the last pragmatist

On 16 March, Ali Larijani — former Speaker of Parliament, four-time nuclear negotiator, and the most prominent moderate left in the Iranian regime — was killed in Tehran. Larijani was an extreme figure by any Western standard, but he was the one senior leader who had historically been willing to come to the table. His death removes the last credible channel through which a negotiated pause might have been brokered.

The remaining Iranian leadership — hardline IRGC commanders and clerics who rose through the war — have no history of diplomacy and no political incentive to negotiate. With no ceasefire in sight and fresh attacks on Saudi shipping, the pathway to negotiations has effectively closed. The oil price has not yet priced this in.

What happens next

No exit, no ceasefire — and a price that keeps rising

Neither side has an offramp. Iran cannot concede without regime collapse — backing down after absorbing the largest strike campaign since World War Two would end the Islamic Republic. America cannot sustain an indefinite forward presence in the Gulf. And Israel will not accept enriched uranium remaining in Iranian hands — somewhere in the country, dispersed facilities survived the opening strikes. That single fact makes a negotiated peace almost impossible to construct.

Iranian drones — cheap, replaceable, and produced in facilities that are difficult to fully destroy — are not going away. Each week the war continues is another week of disrupted shipping, another week of oil priced at crisis levels, another week of the pass-through working its way to the pump.

The price at the pump is not just a cost-of-living number. It is a real-time signal of a war reshaping the Middle East — its borders, its regimes, and the lives of the people living there. Each cent added to the litre price in New Zealand reflects another week of a conflict with no visible end.

Understanding the numbers

The lag effect

Oil is bought 2–4 weeks before it arrives at the pump. The March 13 price of $2.82/L reflects crude bought before the sharpest price moves. Expect further rises through late March and April.

Fixed taxes cushion — partially

Around 77c per litre is fixed taxes — fuel excise, ETS levy, ACC — that don't move with oil. This acts as a buffer, but also means the base price is already high before the oil cost is added.

The NZD amplifies the shock

Dubai crude is priced in USD. The NZD/USD rate has fallen from 0.633 to 0.589 since the war began — adding extra cost on top of the crude price rise itself.

Where this goes next

If the Hormuz closure persists, analysts expect crude to test $130–150/bbl. That would push NZ pump prices above $3.20/L — a record, and roughly double the pre-war level of recent years.

What this means

Three things the data is telling us

01
The Hormuz closure is the bigger unknown.Crude at $115/bbl was already a significant shock. Rerouting tankers around the Cape of Good Hope adds 10–15 days to shipping time and significant freight cost. If the closure persists, price discovery is still underway — none of this is yet in MBIE data.
02
NZ's renewable electricity grid provides partial insulation.Around 80–85% of NZ's electricity comes from hydro, wind, and geothermal. Unlike most countries, a fuel price shock doesn't automatically translate into an electricity price shock. The economy is exposed through transport and freight — not through the power grid.
03
The RBNZ faces a difficult trade-off.Higher fuel prices feed into headline inflation. The Reserve Bank was on a path of gradual rate cuts through 2026. A sustained oil price shock complicates that — cutting into an inflationary environment risks embedding expectations. Fuel prices are now a material input to the monetary policy outlook.
