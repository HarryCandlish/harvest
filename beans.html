Can we predict China's war in Taiwan with beans?

A self-defeating data story, and a small argument about what numbers can and cannot do.

Harry Candlish · Data Analyst · March 2026

The People's Liberation Army runs on pork. This is not a metaphor.

Pork accounts for roughly 60% of all meat consumed in China. It is a staple at military mess halls, a cornerstone of the country's strategic food reserves, and the protein source that 1.4 billion people expect to find cheap and available. When pork prices spike, Chinese households notice. When they spike for long enough, the government notices too.

Pigs eat soybeans. More precisely, pigs eat soy meal — the protein-rich residue left after soybean oil is extracted. China produces around 20 million tonnes of soybeans per year. It imports around 100 million more, primarily from Brazil and the United States. Without those imports, there is no cheap pork. Without cheap pork, there is a political problem. Without a political problem, there is less pressure on the CCP.

This is the chain. Follow it backwards and you get a question: if China were planning an invasion of Taiwan — one that would almost certainly trigger US sanctions and cut off American soybean exports — would we see it first in the price of beans?

This is not entirely absurd

Before Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, analysts at several intelligence agencies noticed something. Russia was procuring blood bags. Lots of them. Medical blood product orders had spiked in the months prior — not enough to make headlines, but enough to register in procurement databases that a handful of people were quietly watching.

They were right to watch. Blood bags don't respond to diplomatic statements or carefully worded communiqués. They are procured because someone believes they will be needed.

The genre of commodity-signal intelligence is real. Before major military operations, states stockpile. Energy is the obvious indicator — Russia had been quietly filling its gas storage to capacity through 2021. Food, medicine, and the raw inputs to food production are also tracked, by those with the resources and access to track them.

I do not have those resources or that access. I have a laptop, a GBIF API key, and a subscription to the CBOT data feed. I have soybeans.

Below is the chart I built. CBOT soybean futures — the global benchmark price — from 2018 to early 2026. Annotated with every event that, depending on your priors, is either significant or irrelevant.

A few things worth noting about what you've just seen.

The biggest spike in the chart has nothing to do with Taiwan. It is the commodity supercycle of 2021–22: COVID supply disruptions, the Ukraine war's effect on fertiliser prices, and China resuming large-scale US purchases after the Phase 1 trade deal. The market was pricing global food insecurity. Not Taiwanese sovereignty.

The events most directly related to Chinese military intent — Xi's 2021 reunification speech, the Pelosi visit and the PLA exercises that followed, Lai Ching-te's election win in January 2024 — produce almost no visible movement in the price. The market looks at Taiwan and, so far, shrugs.

There are two ways to read this. Either the market does not believe an invasion is imminent, and is pricing accordingly. Or the market is wrong — watching the wrong signal — and the preparation is happening in a way that soybean futures simply cannot see.

The problem with this approach

If I were doing this properly — if I had the budget of an intelligence agency rather than the budget of a freelance data journalist — I would be tracking more than soybeans.

I would be tracking iron ore and steel imports, which spike before military hardware production scales up. I would be tracking semiconductor manufacturing equipment, where China has been attempting to front-run US export controls. I would be tracking pharmaceutical procurement, shipping container movements at Fujian ports, and the volume of PLA logistics exercises in the months before any given exercise becomes public knowledge.

I have soybeans.

And here is the uncomfortable truth at the centre of this piece: if China has decided to invade Taiwan, they have also decided to stop importing American soybeans — because the sanctions would end that trade regardless. Which means the signal I am watching might already be priced in as zero. The beans are not spiking because the market has concluded, rationally, that if an invasion happens, US-China soybean trade ends anyway. The futures price is not a predictor. It may already be the aftermath.

I built the wrong chart. I am publishing it anyway, because I think the wrongness is the point.

There is something faintly obscene about this exercise. Not the absurdity — the absurdity is honest, maybe the most honest thing about it. It is the underlying assumption: that if we find the right signal, we will see it coming in time to do something about it. That the most consequential geopolitical event of our generation is, at some level, legible to someone with a data subscription and a free afternoon.

Taiwan's most recent election returned a president that Beijing considers a separatist. 23 million people live on that island. They are not, in the main, watching soybean futures for the all-clear. They are making decisions — where to live, whether to leave, whether the risk is real — on the basis of something that does not appear in any dataset I have access to.

The data journalism I do — including the Taiwan scenario model elsewhere in this portfolio, with its four decision points and its compounding consequence trees — is doing something similar to this piece. Making navigable something that is not navigable. Imposing a decision framework on a situation where the decisions are being made by a 70-year-old man in Beijing whose reasoning we cannot access and whose risk tolerance we cannot model.

The soybean chart is just more honest about its own limitations. It shows you exactly what it cannot tell you. Most data stories don't do that.

The beans don't know.
The chart doesn't care.
Someone in Taipei is having a completely different conversation about this —
one that doesn't involve a graph at all.
